Abstract

Manual crack inspection on bridge decks, pavements, and retaining walls is slow and inconsistent between inspectors. This paper trains a small convolutional network to classify concrete surface patches as cracked or non-cracked, using the public Structural Defects Network (SDNET)-style dataset of 56,092 labeled images spanning decks, walls, and pavements.

The model reaches 89.9% accuracy on a held-out test set, but the headline number hides an important detail: the dataset is heavily skewed toward non-cracked patches, and the model's recall on the crack class itself is only 45%. Section 6 covers this directly rather than around it.

Model — CrackCNN

A plain convolutional stack, built for a two-class problem rather than borrowed from a pretrained backbone. Three convolutional blocks double the channel count each time and halve the spatial size with max pooling, then a single hidden layer and a dropout of 0.5 feed the final classifier.

06

Discussion — the number that matters more than accuracy

89.9% accuracy looks strong until it's split by class. Of the 1,273 truly cracked test images, the model only caught 573 — it missed 700, calling them clean. On the majority class it's excellent: 6,992 of 7,141 non-cracked images correctly identified. A model this confident on the wrong side of an inspection isn't a small detail; a missed crack is the entire point of building this in the first place.

This is the direct fingerprint of the roughly 5.6-to-1 class imbalance from Section 1 flowing through, uncorrected, to the loss function. Cross-entropy without class weights lets the network minimize loss by leaning on the majority class, and it does exactly that.

Reasonable next steps, in rough order of effort: weight the loss (or oversample the crack class) so misses are penalized more heavily; add crack-preserving augmentation (contrast, blur, JPEG noise) since hairline cracks are the failure mode; and compare against a pretrained backbone — a ResNet or EfficientNet stem, fine-tuned — which SDNET-style benchmarks typically report as recovering most of the missing crack recall.

07

Conclusion

A three-block CNN, trained from scratch for ten epochs, gets most of the way to a usable crack classifier — strong on the easy majority class, weak on the class the whole task exists to catch. That gap, not the top-line accuracy, is the honest measure of where this model stands and what to fix next.
